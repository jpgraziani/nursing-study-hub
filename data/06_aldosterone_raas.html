Foundation 6 of 8 — Aldosterone & RAAS

🔁 Aldosterone & the RAAS

The RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System) is the body's emergency blood pressure rescue system. You already have the RAAS diagram in your A&P notes — this note explains how aldosterone fits into that cascade and why it matters for every patient with heart failure, hypertension, or kidney disease.

What aldosterone does — one rule
🧂
Aldosterone tells the kidney: keep sodium, dump potassium
Aldosterone is a hormone made by the adrenal glands (two small glands sitting on top of the kidneys). It acts on the distal convoluted tubule (DCT) — from note 4 — and does one specific swap:

Sodium (Na⁺) gets pulled back into the blood. Potassium (K⁺) gets pushed out into the urine.

Why does this raise blood pressure? Because where sodium goes, water follows (note 1). More sodium retained = more water retained = more blood volume = higher blood pressure.

This is exactly why high aldosterone causes hypertension AND hypokalemia at the same time — and why low aldosterone causes hypotension AND hyperkalemia at the same time.
The full RAAS cascade — step by step
1
KIDNEYS
Blood pressure drops → Kidneys release Renin
JG cells in the kidney detect low pressure, low sodium, or increased sympathetic signals. They release renin — the enzyme that starts the whole cascade.
2
LIVER
Renin converts Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I
The liver makes angiotensinogen constantly. Renin cuts it into angiotensin I — still inactive, but one step closer.
3
LUNGS — where ACE inhibitors act
ACE converts Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II
ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) in the lungs activates Angiotensin II — the powerful active form. ACE inhibitors block this step.
4
BLOOD VESSELS + ADRENAL GLANDS
Angiotensin II acts — vasoconstriction + aldosterone release
Angiotensin II squeezes blood vessels (raises BP immediately) and signals the adrenal glands to release aldosterone. ARBs block angiotensin II receptors here.
5
KIDNEYS (DCT) — where spironolactone acts
Aldosterone → retain Na⁺, excrete K⁺ → BP rises
Aldosterone hits the DCT: Na⁺ retained → water follows → volume rises → BP rises. K⁺ dumped into urine. Spironolactone blocks aldosterone right here.
The drugs that target the RAAS
ACE Inhibitors — Block Step 3
lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril — anything ending in -pril
Block ACE in the lungs → angiotensin I never becomes angiotensin II → no vasoconstriction, less aldosterone, lower BP and less fluid retention. Used for hypertension, heart failure, and post-MI protection.
⚠ Side effect: dry persistent cough — ACE also breaks down bradykinin. When blocked, bradykinin builds up and irritates airways. Not dangerous but annoying enough that patients stop taking it.
⚠ Monitor: hyperkalemia and rising creatinine — less aldosterone = less K⁺ excreted. Check K⁺ and kidney function after starting.
ARBs — Block Step 4
losartan, valsartan, irbesartan — anything ending in -sartan
Angiotensin II is still made, but can't bind to its receptor. Same BP-lowering benefit as ACE inhibitors without the cough — bradykinin is unaffected. Preferred for patients who can't tolerate ACE inhibitor cough.
⚠ Monitor: same hyperkalemia and creatinine risks as ACE inhibitors. Never give ACE inhibitor and ARB together.
Spironolactone — Block Step 5
spironolactone, eplerenone — aldosterone antagonists / K⁺-sparing diuretics
Blocks aldosterone receptors in the DCT → sodium is NOT retained → fluid is removed → BP drops. Because the Na⁺/K⁺ swap is blocked, potassium is NOT lost — hence "potassium-sparing."
⚠ Monitor: hyperkalemia — K⁺ is retained instead of excreted. Never give with other K⁺-sparing drugs or high K⁺ diet without monitoring.
Furosemide (Lasix) — Acts on the Loop of Henle
furosemide (Lasix), bumetanide — loop diuretics
Not technically part of the RAAS but always discussed alongside it. Blocks sodium reabsorption in the Loop of Henle — the most powerful diuretic site. Removes massive amounts of fluid AND potassium.
⚠ Always wastes K⁺ — the opposite of spironolactone. Often given together in heart failure to balance fluid removal with K⁺ conservation.

Addison's Disease — when aldosterone disappears
Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)
The adrenal glands fail and stop making both aldosterone and cortisol. Without aldosterone, the kidneys cannot retain sodium or excrete potassium. Sodium and volume pour out, potassium climbs, blood pressure crashes. An Addisonian crisis is a life-threatening emergency with refractory hypotension that won't respond to IV fluids alone — the hormones must be replaced.
Profound hypotension Low Na⁺ High K⁺ Extreme fatigue Hyperpigmentation Nausea, vomiting
🩺 What the nurse does
1
IV hydrocortisone (cortisol replacement) — given immediately in a crisis. This is the most urgent intervention
2
Fludrocortisone (aldosterone replacement) — oral, given daily for long-term management. Restores the Na⁺/K⁺ balance
3
IV normal saline — restores volume. The BP won't respond until hormones are replaced
4
Monitor K⁺ closely — as aldosterone is replaced, K⁺ will start dropping. Watch for overcorrection
🧠 Memory tricks
"Aldosterone = Salt and Dump" — keeps Salt (Na⁺), Dumps K⁺."

"-pril = ACE inhibitor, -sartan = ARB" — the drug endings tell you the class. Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril. Losartan, valsartan, irbesartan.

"ACE inhibitors cause a dry cough because of bradykinin buildup" — the cough is the most tested ACE inhibitor side effect. When it happens, switch to an ARB.

"Spironolactone and ACE inhibitors both raise K⁺" — both reduce aldosterone effect. Never give together without careful K⁺ monitoring.

"Addison's = No aldosterone = Low Na⁺ + High K⁺ + Low BP" — the triple that defines it.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — Aldosterone & RAAS
ACE inhibitor + ARB = never together — double RAAS blockade causes dangerous hypotension and hyperkalemia. NCLEX will test this.
ACE inhibitor dry cough = expected, not an allergy — teach the patient this is a known side effect. Switch to ARB if intolerable, but do not label it an allergic reaction.
Spironolactone + ACE inhibitor = monitor K⁺ — both retain potassium. Together they risk dangerous hyperkalemia especially in kidney disease.
Furosemide + spironolactone together — a classic heart failure combination. Lasix removes fluid (and K⁺), spironolactone blocks aldosterone (and keeps K⁺). They balance each other out.
Addisonian crisis = give steroids first — hydrocortisone IV is the priority intervention. Fluids alone will not fix the blood pressure without cortisol replacement.
