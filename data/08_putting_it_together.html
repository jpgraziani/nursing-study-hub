🧩 Putting It All Together

You now have all the pieces. This final note connects them into one complete picture — showing how sodium, potassium, the kidneys, ADH, aldosterone, and acid-base all talk to each other in a living patient. After this, the simulator will make complete sense.

The system at a glance
THE
KIDNEYS
🫘
Na⁺ (Note 2)
Kidneys decide how much to keep vs. excrete. Water follows Na⁺ everywhere it goes.
K⁺ (Note 3)
Kidneys are the only real exit for K⁺. In failure, K⁺ builds up and the heart goes into fatal arrhythmia.
ADH (Note 5)
Acts on collecting duct. High ADH = keep water (SIADH). Low ADH = dump water (DI).
Aldosterone (Note 6)
Acts on DCT. Keep Na⁺, dump K⁺. RAAS activates it when BP drops.
Acid-Base (Note 7)
Kidneys excrete H⁺ and retain HCO₃⁻. Acidosis raises K⁺. Alkalosis lowers K⁺.
RAAS (Note 6)
Kidneys trigger the whole cascade when BP falls. Renin → Angiotensin II → Aldosterone → Na⁺ and water retained → BP up.
Three real clinical scenarios — fully traced
💊
Patient on Furosemide for 3 days
Heart failure patient — fluid removed but now electrolytes are crashing
1
Furosemide blocks the Loop of Henle → massive Na⁺ and water lost in urine → fluid volume drops → BP starts falling
2
Furosemide also wastes K⁺ → hypokalemia develops → flat T waves appear on monitor
3
Low BP triggers RAAS → aldosterone rises → kidneys try to retain Na⁺ but also dump more K⁺ → hypokalemia worsens
4
Low volume also triggers ADH → water retained → urine output drops
Nurse's response: IV normal saline to restore volume → KCl replacement (slow IV) → check magnesium → consider holding further Lasix → monitor K⁺ every 4–6 hours → watch cardiac monitor for rhythm changes.
🧠
Post-neurosurgery patient — sudden massive urine output
Hours after brain surgery — urine output climbs to 400 mL/hr
1
Surgery damaged the pituitary → ADH production stops → collecting duct water channels never open
2
Water pours out in massive dilute urine → blood concentrates → Na⁺ rising (hypernatremia)
3
Patient becomes intensely thirsty. If they can't drink fast enough → dehydration and dangerous hypernatremia
4
Brain cells shrink as water is pulled out → confusion, seizure risk
Nurse's response: measure urine output hourly → check specific gravity (will be below 1.005) → notify provider → give DDAVP (synthetic ADH) → replace free water with D5W → monitor Na⁺ closely → correct slowly (no faster than 10–12 mEq/L per 24 hours).
🩸
Type 1 diabetic brought in unconscious — DKA
No insulin for 2 days — everything going wrong at once
1
No insulin → glucose can't enter cells → cells starve → break down fat → ketones produced → metabolic acidosis
2
Acidosis pushes K⁺ out of cells → serum K⁺ looks high (even though total body K⁺ is depleted from vomiting and osmotic diuresis)
3
High blood glucose acts as an osmotic diuretic → massive urine output → dehydration → RAAS fires → aldosterone rises
4
Lungs compensate for acidosis → Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid) to blow off CO₂ → this is a clinical sign you'll recognize at the bedside
5
Give insulin → acidosis corrects → K⁺ rushes back into cells → serum K⁺ crashes → hypokalemia → fatal arrhythmia risk
Nurse's response: IV normal saline to restore volume → start insulin drip → check K⁺ before and continuously during insulin therapy → replace K⁺ aggressively → monitor pH hourly via ABG → watch cardiac monitor → never give insulin if K⁺ below 3.5 without replacing first.

What each simulator scenario means — now that you know the foundation
💊 3 Days on Furosemide
Loop diuretic → wasted K⁺ + dropped volume → RAAS fired → high aldosterone → more K⁺ lost. Fix: NS to restore volume + KCl to replace K⁺
🧠 SIADH
ADH too high → free water retained → Na⁺ diluted → brain cells swell. Fix: fluid restrict to concentrate Na⁺ back up
⚠️ Addison's Crisis
No aldosterone → Na⁺ lost + K⁺ retained + volume crashed + BP unresponsive to fluids. Fix: fludrocortisone + NS + monitor K⁺ as it drops
🩸 DKA
Metabolic acidosis → K⁺ pushed out of cells → looks high. Insulin fixes acid → K⁺ crashes. Fix: NS + insulin + aggressive K⁺ replacement before K⁺ drops
💧 Diabetes Insipidus
No ADH → water pours out → Na⁺ rises → brain cells shrink. Fix: DDAVP + D5W to replace free water slowly
🎯 Final NCLEX Priority — The Big Rules
Never correct Na⁺ or K⁺ too fast — the correction kills. Max Na⁺ correction is 10–12 mEq/L per day. KCl always slow IV, never bolus.
Furosemide = always check K⁺ first — and always have a plan to replace it.
Peaked T waves = call the provider immediately — hyperkalemia with EKG changes is a cardiac emergency. Calcium gluconate first.
Urine output below 30 mL/hr = always notify — it's the earliest warning sign of deterioration.
Kussmaul breathing = metabolic acidosis — the lungs are compensating. Think DKA or kidney failure.
SIADH = fluid restrict (not salt) — Na⁺ is diluted, not depleted. Adding Na⁺ without restricting water doesn't work.
ACE inhibitor dry cough ≠ allergy — switch to ARB. Do not document as allergic reaction.
In DKA, never give insulin if K⁺ is below 3.5 — insulin will drive K⁺ into cells and the patient can arrest from hypokalemia before the DKA is corrected.
