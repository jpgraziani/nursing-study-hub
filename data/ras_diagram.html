Renin-Angiotensin System

🫀 Renin-Angiotensin System

How your body rescues falling blood pressure — step by step

📉
Step 1 — Trigger
Blood Pressure Drops
The kidneys detect low blood pressure, low sodium levels, or increased sympathetic nervous system signals. Something is wrong — the body needs to act.
🫘
Step 2 — Kidney
Renin Released
Juxtaglomerular (JG) cells in the kidney release renin — an enzyme that kicks the whole cascade off. Think of renin as the "alarm" signal.
🏭
Step 3 — Liver
Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I
The liver constantly makes angiotensinogen (inactive). Renin acts on it like scissors, cutting it into Angiotensin I — still inactive, but closer.
🫁
Step 4 — Lungs (ACE)
Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II
ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) in the lungs activates Angiotensin I into Angiotensin II — the powerful, active form. This is where ACE inhibitor drugs work.
⚡
Step 5 — Angiotensin II Acts
Two Powerful Effects
Angiotensin II is a potent vasoconstrictor — it squeezes blood vessels to raise BP immediately. It also travels to the adrenal glands to trigger aldosterone release.
🧂
Step 6 — Adrenal Glands
Aldosterone Released
Aldosterone signals the kidneys to retain sodium and water. More fluid in the blood = more volume = higher blood pressure. It also causes potassium excretion.
✅
Result
Blood Pressure Restored
BP rises from vasoconstriction + fluid retention. The kidneys detect normal pressure — the system shuts off. This feedback loop runs 24/7 automatically.
⚡ What Angiotensin II Does
🩸
Vasoconstriction
Squeezes arterioles → raises BP fast. Direct effect, no middleman.
🧂
Aldosterone Release
Kidneys retain Na⁺ + H₂O → more blood volume → higher BP.
🧠
ADH Release
Signals the brain to release ADH → more water reabsorbed by kidneys.
💧
Thirst Signal
Stimulates thirst center in the brain → you drink more → more fluid volume.
💊 How Drugs Target This System
🚫
ACE Inhibitors — Block Step 4
lisinopril · enalapril · ramipril (-pril ending)
Block ACE in the lungs → Angiotensin I never becomes Angiotensin II → less vasoconstriction, less aldosterone, lower BP. Side effect: dry cough (ACE also breaks down bradykinin — when blocked, bradykinin builds up and irritates airways).
🔒
ARBs — Block Angiotensin II Receptors
losartan · valsartan · irbesartan (-sartan ending)
Angiotensin II is still made, but can't bind to its receptor → same benefit as ACE inhibitors without the cough (bradykinin is unaffected).
💊
Aldosterone Antagonists — Block Step 6
spironolactone · eplerenone
Block aldosterone receptors in the kidney → sodium and water are NOT retained → lower fluid volume → lower BP. Also called potassium-sparing diuretics because potassium isn't lost.
⭐ NCLEX Priority
ACE inhibitors and ARBs are both used for hypertension, heart failure, and post-MI — they reduce cardiac workload by lowering both BP and fluid volume. Monitor for hyperkalemia (aldosterone is suppressed, so less potassium is excreted). Monitor kidney function — if kidneys depend on angiotensin II to maintain GFR (like in renal artery stenosis), blocking the system can cause acute kidney injury.
