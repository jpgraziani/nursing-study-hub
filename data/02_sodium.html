Foundation 2 of 8 — Sodium (Na⁺)

🧂 Sodium (Na⁺)

Sodium is the most important electrolyte for a nurse to understand. It controls where water goes in your body — and water going to the wrong place is what causes almost every serious complication.

Normal range 135 – 145 mEq/L
The one rule that explains everything
🧲
Sodium is a magnet for water
Wherever sodium goes, water follows. This is the single most important thing to understand about sodium.

Your kidneys use this fact to control blood pressure every second of every day. More sodium in the blood = more water pulled into the blood = higher blood volume = higher blood pressure.

Less sodium in the blood = less water pulled in = lower volume = lower blood pressure.

This is exactly why a low-sodium diet helps hypertension. And why IV saline raises blood pressure in a patient who is dehydrated and crashing.

Brain cells are inside a rigid skull. When they swell or shrink, pressure builds fast — which is why sodium problems cause headache, confusion, seizures, and coma.
When sodium is too low — Hyponatremia
📉
Hyponatremia
Na⁺ below 135 mEq/L
Low sodium means there is too much water relative to sodium — the blood is diluted. Water rushes into cells to try to balance things out. Cells swell. Brain cells swelling inside the skull = pressure = neurological symptoms.

Important: hyponatremia is usually not about losing sodium. It's about having too much water diluting the sodium that's there. This is why the fix is often removing water — not adding salt.
Headache Nausea Confusion Lethargy Seizures Coma (severe)
Common causes
Too much plain water / IV D5W — dilutes sodium without replacing it
SIADH — the body retains too much free water due to excess ADH hormone (you'll learn this in note 5)
Heart failure, liver failure, kidney failure — fluid backs up and dilutes sodium
Prolonged vomiting or diarrhea — losing fluid that contains sodium
Diuretics (especially thiazides) — cause sodium loss through urine
🩺 What the nurse does
1
Fluid restrict if the cause is dilutional (too much water) — removing water raises Na⁺ concentration
2
Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) only for severe symptomatic cases (seizures, coma) — given very slowly because raising Na⁺ too fast causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (permanent brain damage)
3
Monitor neurological status — confusion and seizure risk. Safety precautions, fall prevention
4
Treat the underlying cause — if SIADH, address the ADH problem. If heart failure, address fluid overload
When sodium is too high — Hypernatremia
📈
Hypernatremia
Na⁺ above 145 mEq/L
High sodium means there is too little water relative to sodium — the blood is concentrated. Water is pulled OUT of cells to try to balance things out. Cells shrink. Brain cells shrinking = they pull away from the skull = tearing of blood vessels = bleeding.

The most common cause is simply dehydration — not drinking enough water, or losing water without replacing it. Patients who can't access water (confused, intubated, infants, elderly) are most at risk.
Intense thirst Dry mouth Confusion Muscle twitching Seizures Coma (severe)
Common causes
Not drinking enough water — dehydration, especially in patients who can't self-hydrate
Diabetes insipidus — no ADH means massive water loss through urine (you'll learn this in note 5)
Excessive sweating, fever, diarrhea — water lost without adequate replacement
Too much IV normal saline — adding sodium without enough free water
Tube feeding without enough free water — formulas are high in solutes
🩺 What the nurse does
1
Give free water — oral water if the patient can drink, or IV D5W (dextrose in water — the dextrose gets metabolized and only pure water remains)
2
Correct slowly — dropping Na⁺ too fast causes water to rush into brain cells, causing cerebral edema. The goal is no faster than 10–12 mEq/L per 24 hours
3
Monitor neurological status — same as hyponatremia, the brain is at risk either way
4
Identify and treat the cause — if diabetes insipidus, give DDAVP. If dehydration, ensure ongoing fluid access

🧠 Memory tricks that stick
"Salt sucks water" — high sodium sucks water out of cells. Low sodium lets water rush in.

"Hypo = swollen cells, Hyper = shrunken cells" — both cause brain symptoms but for opposite reasons.

"Correct slowly or cause damage" — fixing sodium too fast in either direction causes a different brain injury than the one you're trying to treat. The correction rate matters just as much as the correction itself.

"The thirsty patient has high sodium" — intense thirst is the body's first alarm for hypernatremia. A confused patient who keeps asking for water needs a sodium check.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — Sodium
Both hypo and hypernatremia cause neurological symptoms — always assess neuro status (orientation, level of consciousness) when sodium is abnormal
Never correct sodium rapidly — NCLEX will test this. Too-fast correction of hyponatremia = osmotic demyelination syndrome. Too-fast correction of hypernatremia = cerebral edema. Both are worse than the original problem.
Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) is dangerous — requires an ICU, a central line, and continuous monitoring. Never given fast.
Seizure precautions — any patient with Na⁺ below 125 or above 158 needs seizure precautions, padded side rails, oxygen at bedside
D5W is free water — even though it looks like sugar water, the dextrose metabolizes and what remains is pure water. Used to dilute high sodium, not to feed the patient.
