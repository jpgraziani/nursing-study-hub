Foundation 1 of 8 — What Are Electrolytes?

⚗️ What Are Electrolytes?

Start here. Everything else in fluid balance builds on this one page.

The big picture first
🫧
Your body is basically a bag of saltwater
About 60% of your body weight is water — and it's not pure water. It's water with things dissolved in it. Minerals, salts, charged particles. Those dissolved particles are called electrolytes.

Think of it like this: plain water doesn't conduct electricity. But saltwater does. Your body runs on electrical signals — your heart beating, your nerves firing, your muscles contracting. Electrolytes are what make those electrical signals possible.

When the balance of electrolytes is off, electrical signals go wrong. That's why electrolyte problems show up as heart arrhythmias, muscle weakness, confusion, and seizures — all electrical or muscle-driven symptoms.

What is osmosis? (You need this concept)
Water always moves toward where there is more stuff dissolved in it. If one side of a membrane has more sodium, water flows toward that side to dilute it and even things out.

This is why sodium controls where water goes in your body. Sodium is like a magnet for water. Where sodium goes, water follows. This is the single most important concept in all of fluid balance.
The key electrolytes — what each one does
Na⁺
Sodium
Controls where water goes in the body. The most important electrolyte for blood pressure and fluid balance.
Normal: 135–145 mEq/L
K⁺
Potassium
Controls how heart muscle and nerve cells fire. Even small changes cause big problems with heart rhythm.
Normal: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
Ca²⁺
Calcium
Muscle contraction, bone structure, blood clotting. Low calcium causes muscle spasms and tetany.
Normal: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL
Mg²⁺
Magnesium
Supports hundreds of enzyme reactions. Low magnesium makes it nearly impossible to correct low potassium.
Normal: 1.5–2.5 mEq/L
There are others (phosphate, chloride, bicarbonate) but Na⁺ and K⁺ are what you'll focus on most as a nurse. The rest build on those two.
Why does this matter as a nurse?
❤️
Electrolytes control the heart
Potassium is what makes heart muscle cells reset after each beat. Too much or too little and the heart can't keep a normal rhythm. This is why K⁺ is the electrolyte nurses watch most carefully in the ICU. A K⁺ of 6.5 is a life-threatening emergency.
🧠
Electrolytes control the brain
When sodium is too low, water rushes into brain cells and they swell. A swelling brain inside a rigid skull causes crushing pressure. This is why low sodium causes confusion, seizures, and can be fatal if not caught.
💪
Electrolytes control muscles
Every muscle in your body — including the diaphragm that controls breathing — needs the right electrolyte balance to contract and relax. Severe hypokalemia can cause respiratory muscle weakness and respiratory failure.
🫘
The kidneys control it all
Your kidneys filter your entire blood volume about 60 times per day. They adjust what gets kept and what gets excreted to keep electrolytes in a razor-thin normal range. When the kidneys fail, every electrolyte goes haywire at once. This is why CKD patients are so complex.
What throws electrolytes off balance?
In a healthy person the kidneys maintain perfect balance automatically. Electrolytes go out of balance when something overwhelms the kidneys' ability to compensate:

Too much output — vomiting, diarrhea, excessive sweating, wound drainage, diuretic medications. You lose electrolytes faster than you can replace them.

Too little input — NPO (nothing by mouth), poor nutrition, inability to eat or drink. Not getting enough in to replace normal daily losses.

The kidneys can't do their job — kidney disease, medications that interfere with kidney function, shock reducing blood flow to the kidneys.

Hormones are out of balance — too much or too little aldosterone, ADH, or cortisol changes how the kidneys handle electrolytes. This is what you saw in the simulator.
What to remember from this page
Foundation concepts
1
Electrolytes are charged minerals dissolved in body water. They power electrical signals in the heart, brain, and muscles.
2
⅔ of body fluid is inside cells (K⁺ rules there), ⅓ is outside cells (Na⁺ rules there). They are separated by cell membranes.
3
Sodium is a magnet for water. Where Na⁺ goes, water follows. This controls blood pressure and cell size.
4
Potassium controls heart and nerve firing. Even small changes in K⁺ can cause dangerous heart rhythms.
5
The kidneys are the main regulator. When they fail or are overwhelmed, electrolytes go out of control.
6
Symptoms of electrolyte imbalance are electrical: arrhythmias, muscle weakness, confusion, seizures — because electrolytes run electrical systems.
