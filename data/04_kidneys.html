Foundation 4 of 8 — The Kidneys

🫘 The Kidneys

The kidneys are the master regulators of everything you learned in notes 1–3. Sodium, potassium, fluid volume, acid-base balance — the kidneys control all of it, every second of every day. Understanding what they do makes every other concept click into place.

What the kidneys actually do
🏭
The kidneys are a 24-hour filtration and fine-tuning factory
Your kidneys filter your entire blood volume about 60 times every day — roughly once every 25 minutes. That's about 180 liters of fluid filtered per day, but only 1–2 liters leaves as urine. The rest is carefully reclaimed.

Think of it like a coffee filter — blood goes in, waste and excess gets removed, and everything the body still needs gets reabsorbed back into the bloodstream. But unlike a coffee filter, the kidneys actively decide what to keep and what to throw out based on what the body needs right now.

That decision changes every minute. Blood pressure too high? Kidneys dump more sodium and water into urine. Potassium getting too high? Kidneys excrete more K⁺. Blood getting too acidic? Kidneys excrete acid and retain bicarbonate. This is why kidney failure is so catastrophic — every system breaks at once.
The nephron — the kidney's working unit
Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons — tiny filtering tubes
Bowman's Capsule / Glomerulus — blood is pushed under pressure through tiny capillaries. Water, electrolytes, glucose, and waste all filter out. Large proteins and blood cells stay in the blood.
Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT) — about 65% of filtered sodium, water, glucose, and bicarbonate is reabsorbed here. Almost all glucose is reclaimed (unless blood glucose is too high, like in diabetes).
Loop of Henle — creates a concentration gradient that determines how concentrated the final urine will be. The descending limb allows water out; the ascending limb pumps sodium out. This is exactly where furosemide (Lasix) blocks sodium reabsorption — which is why it's the most powerful diuretic.
Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT) — fine-tuning happens here. Aldosterone acts on this segment — it tells the cells here to swap sodium in for potassium out. Keep Na⁺, dump K⁺.
Collecting Duct — the final decision on water. ADH (vasopressin) acts here — if ADH is present, water channels open and water is reabsorbed back into the blood. If ADH is absent, water stays in the tube and becomes dilute urine.
The six things kidneys regulate
💧
Fluid Volume
Controls how much water stays in the body. More fluid in = more urine out. Less fluid in = concentrated urine to conserve water.
🧂
Sodium (Na⁺)
The main driver of fluid balance. Aldosterone tells kidneys to keep Na⁺. Pressure natriuresis dumps Na⁺ when BP is too high.
⚡
Potassium (K⁺)
Kidneys are the main route for K⁺ excretion. In kidney failure, K⁺ builds up fast — which is why CKD causes dangerous hyperkalemia.
⚗️
Acid-Base (pH)
Kidneys excrete H⁺ (acid) and retain HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate/base). Slower than the lungs but more powerful long-term correction.
🔴
Red Blood Cells
Kidneys produce erythropoietin (EPO) — the hormone that tells bone marrow to make red blood cells. CKD patients become anemic because EPO production drops.
☀️
Vitamin D
Kidneys activate Vitamin D into its usable form (calcitriol). Without this, calcium can't be absorbed from food — leading to bone disease in CKD patients.
What urine tells you about the kidneys
Urine is not just waste — it's a window into what the kidneys are doing right now. As a nurse you will assess urine every shift. Color, amount, concentration, and smell all give you information. The two most important things to track are color and output volume.
🟤
Dark / concentrated urine
Means: kidneys are conserving water. Patient is dehydrated, ADH is high, or fluid volume is low.

Think: not enough fluid in, or too much lost.
💛
Pale / dilute urine
Means: kidneys are excreting water. Patient is well hydrated, ADH is low, or on diuretics.

Think: too much fluid in, or ADH is absent (diabetes insipidus).
Urine output — the number every nurse watches
Normal urine output = 0.5 mL/kg/hour — or about 30–60 mL/hr in adults
Below 30 mL/hr Oliguria ⚠ — call the provider
30–60 mL/hr Normal ✓
Above 100 mL/hr Polyuria — investigate why
Zero output Anuria — kidney failure or obstruction
In the ICU nurses measure urine output every hour using a Foley catheter connected to a measuring bag. A drop in urine output is often the first sign something is wrong — before labs change, before BP drops, before the patient complains of anything.
GFR — how we measure kidney function
GFR = Glomerular Filtration Rate
GFR measures how much blood the kidneys filter per minute. It's calculated from a blood test called creatinine — a waste product from muscle metabolism that healthy kidneys constantly clear. When kidneys fail, creatinine builds up and GFR drops.
Normal
≥ 90 mL/min
Mild CKD
60–89
Moderate CKD
30–59
Severe CKD
15–29
Kidney Failure
Below 15
GFR below 15 = end-stage renal disease (ESRD) — dialysis is needed to do what the kidneys can no longer do.
What happens when the kidneys fail
🚫
Can't excrete potassium → Hyperkalemia
K⁺ builds up in the blood → peaked T waves → risk of cardiac arrest. This happens fast — within hours to days in acute kidney injury.
🚫
Can't excrete acid → Metabolic acidosis
H⁺ accumulates → blood pH drops → acidosis worsens hyperkalemia by shifting more K⁺ out of cells. A vicious cycle.
🚫
Can't excrete fluid → Fluid overload
Water and sodium accumulate → hypertension → pulmonary edema → the patient drowns in their own fluid if not dialyzed.
🚫
Can't activate Vitamin D → Hypocalcemia + bone disease
Inactive Vitamin D → no calcium absorbed from gut → bones are raided for calcium → renal osteodystrophy (bone pain and fractures). This is what the CKD note in your A&P section explains.
🚫
Can't make EPO → Anemia
No erythropoietin → bone marrow stops making red blood cells → fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath. CKD patients need synthetic EPO injections (epoetin alfa).
Fluid balance — what nurses track every shift
Ins and Outs — calculated every shift
IN: IV fluids measured exactly from the IV pump
IN: oral intake estimated from what patient drinks/eats
IN: tube feeding measured from the pump rate
OUT: urine measured from Foley catheter bag
OUT: wound drainage measured from drain bulbs or bags
OUT: GI losses vomiting, diarrhea, NG suction — estimated
What the total means:
Ins = Outs → balanced ✓
Ins much greater than Outs → fluid overload risk
Outs much greater than Ins → dehydration risk
How this connects to everything you've learned
The kidney is the control center for notes 1–3
Sodium (Note 2) — kidneys decide how much Na⁺ to keep vs excrete. Aldosterone tells them to keep it. High blood pressure tells them to dump it (pressure natriuresis).
Potassium (Note 3) — kidneys are the only meaningful route for K⁺ excretion. When they fail, K⁺ has nowhere to go. This is why kidney failure always causes hyperkalemia.
Furosemide (Lasix) — works in the Loop of Henle by blocking the sodium pump there. No sodium reabsorption = sodium and water pour out in urine = powerful diuresis. Now you know exactly where and why.
ADH (coming in Note 5) — acts on the collecting duct to decide how much water to keep. High ADH = water stays = concentrated urine. Low ADH = water pours out = dilute urine. The kidney is the target organ for ADH.
Aldosterone (coming in Note 6) — acts on the DCT to swap Na⁺ in for K⁺ out. The kidney is the target organ for aldosterone. Spironolactone blocks it right here.
🧠 Memory tricks
"60 times a day, 180 liters filtered, 1–2 liters out" — the scale of what kidneys do every day. Most of it gets reclaimed.

"Lasix hits the Loop, Aldosterone hits the DCT, ADH hits the Collecting Duct" — knowing where each drug acts explains why it does what it does.

"Less than 30 mL/hr = call the provider" — oliguria threshold. Never ignore it. It's the first warning sign of kidney trouble or shock.

"When kidneys fail everything fails" — K⁺ rises, pH drops, fluid accumulates, bones weaken, RBCs drop. All at once. This is why CKD patients are the most complex patients you'll care for.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — The Kidneys
Urine output below 30 mL/hr = notify the provider — this is the threshold you must know. No exceptions.
Creatinine rising = kidneys struggling — creatinine is the most reliable lab marker of kidney function. A doubling of creatinine = significant kidney injury.
CKD patients cannot excrete K⁺ — avoid high-potassium foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes) and K⁺-sparing drugs. Hyperkalemia is the most life-threatening CKD complication.
CKD causes anemia — from low EPO, not iron deficiency. Treatment is epoetin alfa (synthetic EPO), not iron supplements alone.
Contrast dye used in CT scans is nephrotoxic — always check kidney function (creatinine/GFR) before giving IV contrast. Patients with CKD need hydration protocols.
NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) are nephrotoxic — teach CKD patients to avoid them. They reduce blood flow to the kidneys and can cause acute kidney injury.
