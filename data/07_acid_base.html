Foundation 7 of 8 — Acid-Base Balance

⚗️ Acid-Base Balance

Your blood's acidity must stay within an extremely narrow range — even small deviations cause heart, brain, and muscle dysfunction. The lungs and kidneys are the two systems that keep pH in balance, and understanding how they work together is essential for reading lab results and understanding sick patients.

The pH scale — what normal means
Blood pH must stay between 7.35 – 7.45. That's a tiny window on a scale of 0–14.

Acidosis = pH below 7.35 — too much acid in the blood. Everything slows down: heart contracts less forcefully, brain function dulls, vasodilation drops BP.

Alkalosis = pH above 7.45 — too little acid (too much base). Nerves become hyperexcitable: muscle spasms, tingling, tetany, seizures. Heart rhythm destabilizes.

The "osis" means the primary problem. The body always tries to compensate. Compensation never fully corrects — it just reduces how far off the pH gets.
The four acid-base disorders
🫁
Respiratory Acidosis
pH ↓ CO₂ ↑
Cause: lungs can't blow off CO₂. CO₂ + H₂O = H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) — blood becomes acidic.
COPD, pneumonia, opioid overdose, hypoventilation, respiratory failure.
Kidneys compensate: retain HCO₃⁻ to buffer the acid. Takes hours to days.
🫁
Respiratory Alkalosis
pH ↑ CO₂ ↓
Cause: breathing too fast blows off too much CO₂. Blood loses acid → becomes alkalotic.
Anxiety, panic attacks, pain, fever, mechanical ventilator set too fast.
Kidneys compensate: excrete HCO₃⁻ to reduce buffering. Takes hours to days.
🫘
Metabolic Acidosis
pH ↓ HCO₃⁻ ↓
Cause: acid builds up or bicarbonate is lost. Kidneys can't excrete acid fast enough.
DKA, kidney failure, lactic acidosis (shock), severe diarrhea (losing HCO₃⁻), aspirin overdose.
Lungs compensate: breathe faster and deeper (Kussmaul respirations) to blow off CO₂ and raise pH. Happens within minutes.
🫘
Metabolic Alkalosis
pH ↑ HCO₃⁻ ↑
Cause: acid is lost or too much bicarbonate is given. Stomach acid is mostly HCl — lose it, blood becomes alkalotic.
Prolonged vomiting, nasogastric suction, excessive antacids, loop diuretics.
Lungs compensate: breathe slower and shallower to retain CO₂ and add acid back. But this is limited — the brain won't let CO₂ rise too high before forcing a breath.
How to quickly tell respiratory from metabolic
There's one simple rule: in respiratory disorders, CO₂ causes the problem. In metabolic disorders, HCO₃⁻ reflects the problem.

CO₂ is an acid — too much makes blood acidic. Too little makes it alkalotic.
HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate) is a base — too much makes blood alkalotic. Too little makes it acidic.

If pH and CO₂ move in OPPOSITE directions → respiratory cause.
(High CO₂ + low pH = respiratory acidosis. Low CO₂ + high pH = respiratory alkalosis.)

If pH and HCO₃⁻ move in the SAME direction → metabolic cause.
(Low HCO₃⁻ + low pH = metabolic acidosis. High HCO₃⁻ + high pH = metabolic alkalosis.)

How lungs and kidneys compensate
Two systems, different speeds
LUNGS
Fast — responds in minutes. Breathe faster to blow off CO₂ (raises pH). Breathe slower to retain CO₂ (lowers pH). The brain stem detects blood acidity and adjusts breathing rate automatically. Compensates for metabolic disorders.
KIDNEYS
Slow — takes hours to days. Excrete H⁺ into urine to remove acid. Retain HCO₃⁻ to add buffer. Or excrete HCO₃⁻ if too alkalotic. Powerful but slow. Compensates for respiratory disorders.
Compensation is never perfect — it reduces the severity but never fully returns pH to 7.40. The body always prioritizes keeping the patient alive over perfect chemistry.
The potassium-pH connection revisited
⚠️ pH always shifts potassium — you saw this in note 3
Now it makes more sense in context. When blood is acidic (too many H⁺ ions floating around), cells try to buffer by pulling H⁺ inside. To maintain electrical balance, K⁺ gets pushed out of cells into the blood → serum K⁺ rises → hyperkalemia.

When blood is alkalotic (too few H⁺), H⁺ moves out of cells. K⁺ moves in to maintain balance → serum K⁺ drops → hypokalemia.

This is why in DKA (diabetic ketoacidosis) — a severe metabolic acidosis — the serum K⁺ looks high before treatment. But when you fix the acidosis with insulin and fluids, K⁺ rushes back into cells and the serum level crashes fast. Always replace K⁺ during DKA treatment.
🧠 Memory tricks
"ROME" — Respiratory Opposite, Metabolic Equal
In Respiratory disorders: pH and CO₂ move in Opposite directions.
In Metabolic disorders: pH and HCO₃⁻ move in Equal (same) directions.

"CO₂ = acid, HCO₃⁻ = base" — keep this anchored and the rest follows logically.

"Lungs = fast, Kidneys = slow" — respiratory compensation happens in minutes. Renal compensation takes hours to days.

"Kussmaul breathing = metabolic acidosis" — deep, rapid, sighing breaths are the lungs trying to blow off CO₂ to compensate. Classic in DKA. NCLEX tests this constantly.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — Acid-Base
Normal pH = 7.35–7.45, CO₂ = 35–45, HCO₃⁻ = 22–26 — memorize these three ranges. Every ABG question starts here.
Kussmaul respirations = metabolic acidosis — the body is trying to blow off CO₂ to compensate. Classic in DKA and kidney failure.
COPD patients have chronically high CO₂ — their kidneys have compensated with high HCO₃⁻. Do not give high-flow oxygen — they rely on hypoxia to drive their breathing. This is tested repeatedly.
Vomiting = metabolic alkalosis — losing stomach acid (HCl) removes H⁺ and raises pH. The nurse replaces volume with normal saline (not D5W) and corrects K⁺.
DKA = metabolic acidosis + fake hyperkalemia — treat acidosis, replace K⁺ before and during insulin therapy. K⁺ will crash when pH is corrected.
