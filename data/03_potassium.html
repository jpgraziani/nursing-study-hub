Foundation 3 of 8 — Potassium (K⁺)

⚡ Potassium (K⁺)

Potassium is the electrolyte nurses are most careful about because even a small change from normal can stop the heart. Understanding it deeply could save someone's life.

Normal range 3.5 – 5.0 mEq/L

Notice how narrow that range is compared to sodium (135–145). A potassium of 2.5 or 6.5 is a medical emergency. A sodium of 125 or 155 is serious but gives you more time. Potassium is less forgiving.

Where potassium actually lives
98%
Inside cells
Intracellular. This is the reservoir — a massive amount stored inside every cell in your body.
2%
In the blood
Extracellular. This tiny amount is what labs measure — and what controls heart rhythm.
⚠️
Why that 2% matters so much
Your lab result shows the K⁺ floating in the blood — just 2% of the total. But it's that 2% that directly controls how heart muscle cells fire. Even a tiny shift in that small pool causes massive electrical changes in the heart.

The other 98% inside cells acts like a buffer. Potassium constantly moves in and out of cells based on pH changes, hormones, and medications. This movement is what makes K⁺ so unpredictable — the serum level you measure is a snapshot, and it can shift fast.
How potassium controls the heart
The resting membrane potential — in plain English
Think of a heart cell like a spring-loaded trap. At rest, the cell is negatively charged inside compared to outside. Potassium is the main ion responsible for maintaining that resting negative charge.

When the heart is supposed to beat, the trap springs — the cell rapidly becomes positive (depolarization), contracts, then resets back to negative (repolarization).

Too little K⁺ (hypokalemia) — the resting charge becomes too negative. The trap is too tightly wound. It becomes unstable and can fire randomly or incompletely.

Too much K⁺ (hyperkalemia) — the resting charge becomes less negative. The trap is too loosely wound. It can't reset properly between beats, causing dangerous arrhythmias.

Either way — wrong potassium = wrong heart rhythm.
What you see on the heart monitor
📖 Reading an EKG strip — the labels
P P wave — the atria (upper chambers) contract to push blood into the ventricles. Small bump before the big spike.
QRS QRS complex — the ventricles (lower chambers) fire and pump blood to the body. The big spike you see on the monitor.
T T wave — the ventricles reset (repolarize) ready for the next beat. The small bump after the spike. This is the one that changes with potassium.
PR PR interval — the pause between atrial and ventricular firing. Widens in hyperkalemia as conduction slows.
✓ Normal K⁺ (3.5–5.0 mEq/L)
Normal rounded T wave after every QRS. The heart fires, pumps, and resets cleanly every cycle. P → QRS → T — steady and regular.
↓ Low K⁺ — Flat T wave + U wave appears
T wave flattens — the ventricle is struggling to repolarize because K⁺ is needed for that reset. U wave appears — a small extra bump after the T, almost like an echo. Classic sign of hypokalemia. Risk of V-fib — the heart can start quivering instead of pumping.
↑ High K⁺ — Peaked T waves → wide QRS → arrest
Tall, peaked, tent-shaped T waves are the first warning sign — the repolarization is too fast and too strong. As K⁺ climbs higher: PR interval lengthens → QRS widens (you can see it getting broader) → eventually forms a sine wave → cardiac arrest. Each change is a step closer to the edge. This is a progressive emergency — act at peaked T waves, do not wait.
When potassium is too low — Hypokalemia
📉
Hypokalemia
K⁺ below 3.5 mEq/L
Low potassium is extremely common in hospitalized patients — especially anyone on diuretics, vomiting, or not eating. The heart and muscles are the first to show it. The lower K⁺ drops, the more dangerous the rhythm becomes. Below 2.5 is a cardiac emergency.
Muscle weakness Leg cramps Fatigue Constipation Flat T waves on EKG U waves on EKG V-fib risk
Common causes
Furosemide (Lasix) and other loop diuretics — most common cause in the hospital. The kidneys dump K⁺ into the urine along with the fluid
Vomiting and nasogastric suction — losing gastric contents removes potassium
Diarrhea — large amounts of K⁺ lost through the GI tract
High aldosterone — aldosterone causes the kidney to excrete K⁺ in exchange for keeping Na⁺
Not eating (NPO, poor intake) — not enough potassium coming in
Alkalosis — when blood pH rises, K⁺ shifts into cells, dropping the serum level
🩺 What the nurse does
1
Replace potassium — oral KCl if patient can swallow and gut works. IV KCl if severe or NPO. Always check the order for rate and concentration
2
NEVER give IV KCl as a bolus — it must always be diluted and infused slowly. Direct injection causes immediate cardiac arrest. This is one of the most critical safety rules in nursing
3
Check magnesium — low magnesium makes hypokalemia impossible to correct. You have to fix Mg²⁺ first or the K⁺ will not stay up no matter how much you give
4
Monitor the heart — continuous cardiac monitoring. Watch for T wave changes and dangerous rhythms
5
Hold or reduce the diuretic — if furosemide caused the problem, discuss with the provider whether to continue it
When potassium is too high — Hyperkalemia
📈
Hyperkalemia
K⁺ above 5.0 mEq/L
High potassium is less common than low potassium but far more immediately dangerous. It can cause cardiac arrest with very little warning. A K⁺ of 6.0 or above is a medical emergency — you notify the provider immediately and prepare to act fast.
Muscle weakness Tingling / numbness Nausea Peaked T waves Wide QRS Cardiac arrest
Common causes
Kidney failure (CKD / AKI) — kidneys can't excrete K⁺, so it builds up in the blood
ACE inhibitors and ARBs — reduce aldosterone → less K⁺ excreted → K⁺ rises
Potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone) — blocks aldosterone, retains K⁺
Acidosis — when blood pH drops, K⁺ shifts OUT of cells into the blood
Tissue destruction (burns, crush injury, hemolysis) — cells rupture and dump K⁺ into the bloodstream
Too much IV potassium — given too fast or too concentrated
🩺 What the nurse does — in order of priority
1
Calcium gluconate IV first — protects the heart immediately by stabilizing the cardiac membrane. Does NOT lower K⁺ — it just buys time. This is always the first step when K⁺ is dangerously high and EKG changes are present
2
Insulin + dextrose IV — insulin drives K⁺ from the blood into cells, lowering the serum level fast. Dextrose prevents hypoglycemia. Effect lasts 4–6 hours
3
Sodium bicarbonate — if the patient is also acidotic, correcting the pH shifts K⁺ back into cells
4
Kayexalate or patiromer — binds K⁺ in the gut so it's excreted in stool. Slower acting — used for less urgent situations
5
Dialysis — the definitive fix when kidneys can't excrete K⁺ on their own. Used in kidney failure
The potassium-pH connection
⚠️ K⁺ and pH always move in opposite directions
↓
Acidosis (low pH) → H⁺ ions move INTO cells → K⁺ gets pushed OUT of cells → serum K⁺ rises → hyperkalemia
↑
Alkalosis (high pH) → H⁺ ions move OUT of cells → K⁺ gets pulled INTO cells → serum K⁺ drops → hypokalemia
This is why DKA patients look like they have high K⁺ — the acidosis pushed K⁺ out of cells. But when you give insulin and fix the acidosis, K⁺ rushes back into cells and the serum level crashes. If you didn't replace K⁺ proactively, the patient can go into fatal hypokalemia right after treatment. This is one of the trickiest clinical situations in nursing.
⛔ The most important safety rule in nursing
Never give potassium chloride (KCl) as an IV push or bolus. Ever. Under any circumstances.

Potassium injected directly into the bloodstream causes immediate cardiac arrest. It is one of the medications on the high-alert list in every hospital. KCl must always be diluted in IV fluid and infused slowly over hours through a pump with a nurse monitoring. Many hospitals require two nurses to verify IV potassium orders before they are given.

🧠 Memory tricks
"Low K⁺ = U waves and V-fib. High K⁺ = peaked T and arrest." — these EKG patterns are high-yield NCLEX.

"Calcium first, then insulin, then dig it out" — the order of hyperkalemia treatment: protect the heart, shift K⁺ into cells, remove it from the body.

"Can't fix K⁺ without fixing Mg²⁺" — if magnesium is low, potassium won't stay in the blood no matter how much you give. Always check both.

"Acidosis pushes K⁺ out, Alkalosis pulls K⁺ in" — pH and K⁺ always move opposite. This comes up constantly.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — Potassium
Never give IV KCl as a bolus — this is THE most tested potassium safety rule. Always diluted, always slow, always on a pump.
Calcium gluconate is always first in hyperkalemia — it does not lower K⁺, it protects the heart while you prepare treatment.
Check magnesium before and during K⁺ replacement — hypomagnesemia makes hypokalemia refractory (won't correct).
DKA K⁺ trap — K⁺ looks high before treatment but crashes after insulin. Always replace K⁺ when treating DKA.
Furosemide patients need K⁺ monitoring every shift — most common cause of hypokalemia in the hospital.
Peaked T waves on the monitor = call the provider NOW — do not wait for symptoms to appear. Arrest can come with little warning.
