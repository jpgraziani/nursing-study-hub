Foundation 5 of 8 — ADH

💧 ADH — The Water Hormone

ADH stands for Antidiuretic Hormone — antidiuretic means "against urination." Its only job is to tell the kidneys how much water to keep. Understanding ADH explains two conditions that look like opposite sodium problems: SIADH and Diabetes Insipidus.

What ADH does — one job, one location
🚰
ADH controls the water tap in the collecting duct
From note 4 you know the collecting duct is the last segment of the nephron — the final decision point for water. ADH is the hormone that opens or closes the water channels in that duct.

ADH present → water channels open → water reabsorbed back into blood → concentrated urine, low output.

ADH absent → water channels closed → water stays in the tube → dilute urine, high output.

That's the entire mechanism. Everything else about ADH is just applying this in different situations.
When does the body release ADH?
Trigger 1
Blood osmolarity rises (blood gets too concentrated)
When sodium or other solutes rise in the blood, sensors in the hypothalamus detect the concentration. The hypothalamus makes ADH and the pituitary gland releases it.
↓
Trigger 2
Blood volume drops (dehydration, blood loss)
Pressure receptors in the heart and blood vessels detect low volume. They signal the brain to release ADH to retain water and restore volume.
↓
Result
Kidneys retain water → blood dilutes → osmolarity drops → ADH turns off
A perfect feedback loop. The body monitors its own concentration and adjusts ADH release accordingly, constantly fine-tuning the water balance.
When ADH is too LOW — Diabetes Insipidus (DI)
Diabetes Insipidus (DI)
ADH absent or ineffective
Without ADH the water channels in the collecting duct never open. Water pours out in massive amounts of very dilute urine — sometimes 3–20 liters per day. The blood becomes dangerously concentrated (hypernatremia) because water is leaving faster than the patient can drink.

Important: despite the word "diabetes," this has nothing to do with blood sugar. The name comes from the massive urine output — similar to diabetes mellitus. "Insipidus" means tasteless (dilute urine), versus "mellitus" which means sweet (glucose in urine).
Massive urine output (polyuria) Extreme thirst (polydipsia) Very dilute pale urine Rising Na⁺ (hypernatremia) Dehydration if can't drink
Common causes
Traumatic brain injury or neurosurgery — damage to the hypothalamus or pituitary disrupts ADH production or release. Classic post-op neurosurgery complication
Brain tumors or meningitis — pressure or inflammation affecting the hypothalamus-pituitary axis
Central DI — the brain doesn't make enough ADH
Nephrogenic DI — the kidneys don't respond to ADH even when it's present. Caused by lithium toxicity or kidney disease
🩺 What the nurse does
1
DDAVP (desmopressin) — synthetic ADH given intranasally, orally, or IV. Replaces what the brain isn't making. Works for central DI but not nephrogenic DI
2
Replace free water — D5W IV or oral water to dilute the rising Na⁺. Monitor Na⁺ closely — correct slowly
3
Measure urine output hourly — volumes over 200–300 mL/hr are a red flag. Strict I&Os are critical
4
Monitor specific gravity — DI urine is very dilute, specific gravity below 1.005. Healthy concentrated urine is 1.010–1.030
When ADH is too HIGH — SIADH
SIADH
Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH Secretion
Too much ADH means the kidneys keep reabsorbing free water even when they shouldn't. The blood becomes diluted — sodium drops (dilutional hyponatremia) even though the body's total sodium hasn't really changed. There's just too much water relative to the sodium.

The name says it all — the ADH is being secreted when it shouldn't be. The body is holding onto water inappropriately. The result is a waterlogged, low-sodium state.
Low Na⁺ (hyponatremia) Concentrated urine (paradox) Fluid overload Headache, confusion Nausea, seizures (severe)
Common causes
Brain conditions — meningitis, brain tumors, stroke, head injury — the brain misfires ADH release
Lung conditions — pneumonia, small cell lung cancer (tumor produces its own ADH), tuberculosis
Medications — SSRIs (antidepressants), certain chemotherapy drugs, carbamazepine, opioids
Pain, nausea, stress — all stimulate ADH release as a survival mechanism — can cause SIADH in post-op patients
🩺 What the nurse does
1
Fluid restriction — first-line treatment. Limiting water intake (often 800–1000 mL/day) allows the kidneys to gradually concentrate the blood and raise Na⁺. No free water IV fluids
2
Monitor Na⁺ closely — goal is to raise Na⁺ slowly (max 10–12 mEq/L per 24 hours). Too fast causes osmotic demyelination syndrome — permanent brain damage
3
Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) — only if Na⁺ is critically low with active seizures. Requires ICU monitoring
4
Treat the underlying cause — stopping the offending medication or treating the lung/brain condition often resolves SIADH
5
Seizure precautions — fall prevention, padded rails, oxygen at bedside when Na⁺ is critically low

🧠 Memory tricks
"ADH = Anti-Diuretic — it's AGAINST urination" — high ADH means less urine. Low ADH means floods of urine. The name tells you everything.

"DI = Dry and high Na⁺. SIADH = Soggy and low Na⁺" — opposite conditions. If you can picture one you can reason out the other.

"SIADH urine is concentrated even though the blood is diluted" — this seems backwards but makes sense: ADH is telling the kidneys to keep water. So blood gets watered down but the little urine that forms is concentrated.

"SSRIs cause SIADH" — antidepressants are one of the most common medication causes. Very NCLEX-testable. Any patient on an SSRI with confusion and low Na⁺ — think SIADH.
🎯 NCLEX Priority — ADH
DI after brain surgery = expected complication — always monitor urine output hourly after neurosurgery. Output over 200–300 mL/hr = notify provider immediately.
SIADH paradox — urine is concentrated BUT blood Na⁺ is low. NCLEX loves testing this seeming contradiction. It makes sense once you know ADH is inappropriately high.
Fluid restriction is first-line for SIADH — not salt tablets, not diuretics. Restrict water so the body gradually concentrates its own sodium.
SSRIs, opioids, carbamazepine — common medication causes of SIADH. Always review the med list when a patient has unexplained hyponatremia.
Specific gravity — DI urine is below 1.005 (very dilute). SIADH urine is above 1.020 (very concentrated). These numbers come up on NCLEX.
